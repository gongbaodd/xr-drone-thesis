\subsubsection{Environment blend modes}

In XR systems, rendered images must be composited with the system display to produce the final user-perceived output. According to \textcite{openxr2024spec}, this process is governed by one of the three environment blend modes: opaque, additive, and alpha blend.

The term environment in this context refers either to the user's perception of the physical world beyond the display or to a fully synthetic environment generated by the system. In many XR applications, it may also represent a combination of both real and virtual elements. Environment blend modes therefore define how virtual content is integrated with, or replaces, the real-world view.

Opaque blending fully replaces the real-world view with virtual imagery and is typically used in virtual reality (VR), where users are immersed in a completely computer-generated environment. In contrast, augmented reality (AR) aims to overlay digital content onto the physical world, aligning virtual elements spatially and perceptually with the real environment \parencite{schmalstieg_augmented_2016}.

Two primary display technologies are commonly used in AR systems: optical see-through (OST) and video see-through (VST). In optical see-through head-mounted displays (HMDs), users view the real world directly through semi-transparent mirrors, onto which virtual imagery is projected. In video see-through systems, the real-world view is captured by cameras and digitally composited with virtual content before being displayed to the user \parencite{rolland_head-mounted_2005, billinghurst_survey_2015}.

These display technologies correspond closely to different blending strategies. Additive blending is typically associated with optical see-through displays, where virtual content is added to the incoming real-world light without fully occluding it. Alpha blending, on the other hand, is commonly used in video see-through systems, where pixel-wise compositing allows for proper occlusion and depth integration between real and virtual elements.

Recent developments in HMDs increasingly rely on video passthrough technology. In such systems, users perceive the physical environment indirectly through stereoscopic, real-time video feeds displayed inside the headset rather than through direct optical transmission \parencite{bailenson2024passthrough}. This approach enables more flexible and accurate compositing of virtual and real-world elements.

Empirical studies have shown that passthrough-based approaches can enhance user experience. For instance, \textcite{waldow_investigating_2025} introduced the concept of Pass-Through Embodiment (PTE) and demonstrated that allowing users to see their own physical bodies in real time significantly improves presence and embodiment without negatively affecting task performance or inducing simulator sickness.

Overall, the choice of environment blend mode and display technology plays a critical role in shaping the perceptual and interaction characteristics of XR applications. Leveraging modern passthrough capabilities can enable more immersive and context-aware mixed reality experiences, which is particularly valuable in application domains such as simulation and training.
